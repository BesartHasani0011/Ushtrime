Tiramisu (Italian: tiramisù [ˌtiramiˈsu], from tirami su, "pick me up" or "cheer me up")[1] is a coffee-flavoured Italian dessert. It is made of ladyfingers (savoiardi) dipped in coffee, layered with a whipped mixture of eggs, sugar, and mascarpone cheese, flavoured with cocoa.

The recipe has been adapted into many varieties of cakes and other desserts.[2] Its origins are often disputed among Italian regions Veneto and Friuli Venezia Giulia.

The recipe for tiramisu is not found in cookbooks before the 1960s.[3][4][5] It is also not mentioned in encyclopedias and dictionaries of the 1970s,[6][7][8] making its first appearance in print in Italian in 1980,[9] and in English in 1982.[10] It is mentioned in a 1983 cookbook devoted to cooking of the Veneto, [11] and a Canadian tourist guide published in 1971.[12] This suggests that it is a recent invention.

Obituaries for the restaurateur Ado Campeol (1928–2021) reported that it was invented at his restaurant Le Beccherie in Treviso on 24 December 1969 by his wife Alba di Pillo (1929–2021) and the pastry chef Roberto Linguanotto.[13][14][15][16] The dish was added to its menu in 1972.[17][18][19][20][21][22][23]
